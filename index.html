The Utah Division of Water Quality (DWQ) works to protect and improve the health of Utah's streams, lakes, wetlands, and groundwater. We are developing a nine-element watershed plan for the White Mesa area — and we cannot do it without you. A watershed plan is built with a community, not handed to one. Your observations and priorities shape what goes in it and help guide future projects, monitoring, and funding opportunities for this watershed.

What a Watershed Plan Does

A watershed plan documents water quality conditions, identifies shared concerns, and outlines voluntary, community-supported actions. You decide what role you play. These plans carry no enforcement authority and cannot impose requirements on your community, tribal lands, or any individual. Instead, they can help prioritize things like protecting important springs, improving wells or infrastructure, or increasing monitoring where you have concerns

About the Uranium Mill

Concerns about groundwater contamination from the White Mesa Mill are real and deeply felt. Regulation of the mill falls under the Utah Division of Radiation Control and EPA — not DWQ. This plan cannot regulate the mill. But your water quality concerns absolutely belong here, and we want to hear them so they can guide monitoring, studies, and future actions in this watershed.

Questions We'd Like to Ask You

There are no right or wrong answers. Lived experience is knowledge. You don't need to be a scientist — what you see, feel, and experience with water in your community matters.

  1. 1What water sources does your community rely on — springs, wells, streams? How have these changed over time?
  2. 2What have you observed about water quality? Any changes in taste, smell, color, or wildlife near water?
  3. 3What are your biggest water quality concerns in the White Mesa watershed, now or in the future?
  4. 4Are there culturally or spiritually significant water places in this watershed that you'd want protected?
  5. 5What traditional knowledge or practices relate to water care and stewardship in your community?
  6. 6What would meaningful watershed protection look like to you and your family?
  7. 7How would you like to stay involved as this process moves forward?

Your community has lived alongside this land and water for generations. What you've seen, what's changed, what worries you — that's exactly what we need to ensure the plan addresses your concerns.

Why We're Here

We're here to learn from you. We want to understand your concerns, your observations, and what matters most to your community about this watershed so the plan reflects your priorities, not just our assumptions.

Five Commitments We're Making to the White Mesa Community

1
We will listen without judgment.

Your concerns and observations are valid — whether or not they fit neatly into a planning framework. We are here to hear you, not to evaluate you, and we will make time for what you want to talk about.

2
We will protect sensitive information.

If you share culturally significant locations or sensitive knowledge, we will work with you on whether and how that information appears in any public document, respecting your community’s decision and cultural sovereignty.

3
We will accurately represent your input.

We will document what we hear and share that documentation with you for review before it is used in any plan. You have the final say on how your words are represented, and we will make changes based on your feedback.

4
We will be honest about our limits.

We cannot regulate the uranium mill. We will not overpromise. We will tell you clearly what this plan can and cannot do — and we will not pretend otherwise. We will also be clear about the tools we do have, such as monitoring, planning, and supporting voluntary projects.

5
We will keep coming back.

This is not a one-time visit. We are committed to ongoing engagement with the White Mesa community throughout the entire planning process — and beyond, including follow-up visits and updates as the plan is developed and implemented.
